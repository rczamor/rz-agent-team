# Shreyas Doshi

## Why they matter to the PM-lite
Doshi is the sharpest modern voice on *how PMs actually execute well* — writing quality, prioritization, why PMs fail. He was Stripe's fourth PM, built their PM function from 5 to 50+, and now teaches PM craft via Maven courses, Twitter/X, and *The Skip* podcast. For PM-lite specifically, Doshi is the closest fit of any source: his work centers on the execution layer (writing crisp artifacts, prioritizing within a day, editing others' proposals) rather than strategy. His LNO (Leverage / Neutral / Overhead) framework is the single most useful tool PM-lite has for allocating its own effort across a packed ticket queue — spend perfectionism on the high-leverage spec, spend "just okay" on the status update. His "most execution problems are strategy problems" insight also gives PM-lite a clean rule: when a ticket keeps ping-ponging, stop trying to fix the ticket and escalate the strategic ambiguity to Riché.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
1. **LNO: not all tasks deserve perfectionism.** Leverage tasks (a PRD for an important feature, aligning two teams) deserve your best work; Neutral tasks (routine specs) deserve solid-but-normal execution; Overhead tasks (recurring status updates) deserve "OK-tier" speed.
2. **Most execution problems are strategy problems.** When a team churns on a ticket, the root cause is usually unresolved strategic ambiguity, not poor tactics. PM-lite should escalate rather than grind.
3. **Three levels of product work: execution, strategic, mission-critical.** PM-lite works at the execution level primarily; it must recognize when a ticket is actually strategic and route it to Riché.
4. **Editing is a PM skill.** Senior PMs don't just write; they critically evaluate and sharpen others' proposals. PM-lite should review engineer-authored tickets the same way.
5. **Pre-mortems beat retrospectives.** Before a ticket or project starts, ask "if this fails in 3 months, why?" — cheaper than learning the hard way.
6. **ROI vs. opportunity cost.** A high-ROI ticket that displaces a higher-ROI one is still a mistake. Always ask what the next-best use of this capacity is.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse

**LNO triage grid (run on PM-lite's own task list each morning):**
| Task | L/N/O | Time budget | Quality bar |
|---|---|---|---|
| Draft spec for auth rewrite | L | 3h deep work | perfectionist |
| Update Linear statuses from standup | O | 10min | functional |
| Notion grooming pass | N | 45min | normal |

Rule: if an L task hasn't had its 3h deep-work slot by 11am, everything else gets bumped.

**Pre-mortem template (paste into Notion before a project kicks off):**
```
If this project fails by <date>, the most likely reasons are:
1. <failure mode> — early signal: <observable>
2. <failure mode> — early signal: <observable>
3. <failure mode> — early signal: <observable>
Pre-commitments: we will stop and replan if <signal> appears.
```

**"Is this strategic or executional?" flag-up rule:**
If a ticket touches any of these, PM-lite does NOT decide — it writes the options memo and hands to Riché:
- A tradeoff between two user segments
- A tradeoff between speed-to-ship and long-term quality
- A pricing/packaging implication
- A "build vs. buy vs. integrate" call

**Edit-pass checklist for tickets authored by others:**
- [ ] Is the outcome stated in one sentence?
- [ ] Are acceptance criteria testable (not aspirational)?
- [ ] Any jargon that obscures the problem?
- [ ] Is the scope smaller than it could be?
- [ ] Would a new hire understand this without asking?

**Opportunity-cost line:** before committing a ticket to a sprint, write one sentence: "By doing this, we are explicitly NOT doing ____." If PM-lite can't fill the blank, the sprint isn't planned.

## Where they disagree with others
- **Doshi vs. RICE / numeric prioritization:** Doshi favors LNO and ROI-vs-opportunity-cost over heavy scoring rituals. Use LNO for daily task allocation, RICE (if anyone asks) for cross-team quarterly planning.
- **Doshi vs. Cagan on "product teams":** Doshi is more realistic that most companies have execution-heavy PM roles (like PM-lite) and gives them tools to thrive, whereas Cagan would call that a "feature team" smell. Doshi is the better mentor for PM-lite's actual scope.
- **Doshi vs. Lenny:** Lenny offers templates; Doshi offers thinking tools. Use both: a Lenny PRD template filled out with a Doshi LNO mindset.
