# Erika Hall

## Why they matter to the Researcher
Erika Hall wrote the single most practical book on research for teams that don't have a dedicated research function — which is exactly the posture the Researcher agent occupies on an 11-agent team. *Just Enough Research* is sharp on interview design, bias detection, and the distinction between research types (organizational, user, competitive, evaluative) that often get collapsed in practice. For the Researcher, Hall's work is the antidote to two failure modes: doing too much ceremonial research that produces nothing usable, and skipping research entirely because "there isn't time." Her framing also reinforces the agent's "present options, don't decide" mandate — Hall is emphatic that research exposes problems and blind spots, not conclusions.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Research is a thinking activity, not a ceremony.** The point is to change your mind, not to produce an artifact. If the report is the goal, you're doing it wrong.
- **Four research types, not one.** Organizational (how does this company actually work?), user/customer (who are they and what are they doing?), competitive (what else exists in this space?), and evaluative (does this thing work?). Each needs its own plan.
- **Bias mitigation starts with the question.** Don't ask people how they feel and treat their answer as fact. Ask about behavior, observe artifacts, and explicitly note where bias is likely in the writeup.
- **Never hold a focus group.** Group dynamics destroy signal. 1:1 interviews and observation are the defaults.
- **Do research together.** Having a second teammate on each interview surfaces different observations and counteracts single-researcher bias.
- **Structured six-step process:** define the problem → select approach → plan/prepare → collect → analyze → report. Skipping "define the problem" is the most common failure.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Research plan one-pager (use for every Notion research page):**
  - Problem being explored (one sentence)
  - Research type (organizational / user / competitive / evaluative)
  - Method and why this method
  - Participants: who, how many, how recruited
  - Questions we expect to answer
  - What we'd do differently based on each possible finding (if you can't answer this, don't run the study)
- **Interview script skeleton (Hall style):**
  1. Set context, get consent, promise no right answers
  2. Background: current role, recent relevant activity
  3. Grand tour: "walk me through…" — open, behavioral
  4. Probes on specific behaviors and artifacts observed
  5. Critical incident: last time something went wrong / went well
  6. Wrap: anything I should have asked?
- **Bias-check checklist on every synthesis:** leading questions flagged, confirmation-bias candidates marked, sample limitations noted, single-source claims called out.
- **Competitive research matrix columns (Hall's cut):** competitor, how they frame the problem, target user, pricing model, primary differentiator, observed gaps, source URL. Keep it descriptive — scoring belongs to the PM.
- **Organizational-research intake:** who decides, who influences, who blocks, what language do they use, what are they incentivized on.

## Where they disagree with others
- Hall vs. NN/g on sample sizes: NN/g's "5 users catches 85%" rule is a heuristic Hall endorses in spirit but she pushes back on anyone treating it as universal — she insists the right sample depends on the question type and population variance.
- Hall vs. Gartner/market-report traditions: she's skeptical of research that produces tidy quadrants, preferring messy observation and named-bias reporting over the appearance of objectivity.
- Hall vs. focus-group-driven consumer research: she treats focus groups as actively harmful and recommends structured 1:1s instead.
