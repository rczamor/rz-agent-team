# Christian Rohrer / Nielsen Norman Group (NN/g)

## Why they matter to the Researcher
NN/g publishes the most rigorous, citable library of UX research methods on the web — when each method applies, what sample sizes are defensible, and how to synthesize results. Christian Rohrer's "When to Use Which User-Experience Research Methods" is the canonical method-selection chart. For the Researcher agent, NN/g is the "show your work" reference: when the agent presents options on a Notion research page, it can cite Rohrer's framework to justify why it chose field study over a survey, or usability testing over analytics. This keeps the Researcher's recommendations defensible under scrutiny without needing to reinvent methodology arguments every time. NN/g's sample-size guidance ("5 users catches ~85% of usability problems") is also a load-bearing heuristic for scoping quick evaluations.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Three axes for method selection.** Attitudinal vs. behavioral (what people say vs. what they do), qualitative vs. quantitative (direct observation vs. indirect measurement), and context of product use (natural, scripted, limited, none). Every method sits somewhere in that cube.
- **Match method to product phase.** Strategize (generative: field studies, interviews) → Design (formative: usability testing, tree testing) → Launch & assess (summative: A/B testing, analytics, surveys).
- **Behavioral beats attitudinal when they conflict.** What people do is more reliable than what people say.
- **5 users is enough for qualitative usability.** Additional users yield diminishing marginal issue-discovery; use the budget for more rounds or more segments instead.
- **Quantitative methods need real sample sizes.** The "5 users" rule is for qualitative formative work only — surveys and A/B tests require statistical sizing.
- **Triangulate.** Pair a qualitative method with a quantitative one when decisions are high-stakes.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Rohrer method-selection grid (copy into every research plan):**
  | Axis | Options | Chosen | Why |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | Attitudinal vs behavioral | attitudinal / behavioral / both | | |
  | Qualitative vs quantitative | qual / quant / mixed | | |
  | Context of use | natural / scripted / limited / none | | |
  | Product phase | strategize / design / assess | | |
- **Method-to-question cheat sheet:**
  - "What do users need?" → field studies, interviews, diary studies
  - "Is the design usable?" → moderated or unmoderated usability testing, 5+ users
  - "How are features performing?" → analytics, A/B testing, surveys
  - "How do they mentally organize X?" → card sorting, tree testing
  - "What do users believe?" → surveys, focus groups (use with caution)
- **Sample-size justification boilerplate:** "Per Nielsen (2000), ~5 users surface ~85% of usability issues for a single user segment. For N segments, plan 5×N sessions."
- **Triangulation checklist:** for any high-stakes recommendation, include at least one behavioral + one attitudinal data source, cite both.

## Where they disagree with others
- NN/g's "5 users is enough" vs. statistical-power critiques: many researchers argue the 85% figure is fragile outside specific assumptions about problem distribution; the Researcher should cite Rohrer's nuance (5 users per segment, formative only) rather than the bumper-sticker version.
- NN/g vs. Torres: NN/g frames method selection as the central craft; Torres treats story-based interviews as the default and invests rigor in synthesis structure. Both are compatible but emphasize different work.
- NN/g vs. Hall: Hall is more skeptical of method orthodoxy and more willing to improvise; NN/g defaults to method rigor and documentation.
