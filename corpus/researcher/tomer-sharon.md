# Tomer Sharon

## Why they matter to the Researcher
Tomer Sharon is the most practical voice on lean user research and research-ops at scale. He has led user research at Google Search, WeWork, and Goldman Sachs, and his books are structured around the real questions product teams ask ("will they buy it?", "can they use it?") rather than around research methods in the abstract. For the Researcher agent, Sharon matters on two fronts: (1) *Validating Product Ideas* provides a question-first method picker — useful when the Researcher is deciding how to tackle a Notion research page without overbuilding; and (2) *It's Our Research* is the canonical text on stakeholder buy-in, which is exactly how the Researcher avoids becoming a silo and how it earns its "present options, don't decide" posture with the rest of the team.

## Signature works & primary sources
- *Validating Product Ideas: Through Lean User Research* (Rosenfeld Media, 2016) — https://rosenfeldmedia.com/books/lean-user-research/ — Question-first research playbook for practitioners without heavy UX training.
- *It's Our Research: Getting Stakeholder Buy-in for User Experience Research Projects* (Morgan Kaufmann, 2012) — https://shop.elsevier.com/books/its-our-research/sharon/978-0-12-385130-7 — Playbook for involving stakeholders across the whole research lifecycle.
- *Measuring User Happiness* (2020) — Applies the HEART framework and related metrics to post-launch evaluation.
- Medium essays and LinkedIn posts — ongoing commentary on research-ops and democratization.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Start with the question, not the method.** Lean User Research is organized by the questions product teams actually ask; the method is a consequence, not a starting choice.
- **Democratize, don't gatekeep.** Teach stakeholders to do "far-from-robust, quick-and-dirty" research themselves rather than hoarding research as a specialist function.
- **Research is "ours," not "mine."** Stakeholders are co-authors of the research plan, not audiences for a final report. This is the thesis of *It's Our Research* and is how impact actually happens.
- **Observe behavior, not self-reports.** Sharon aligns with Hall here: watch what people do, don't collect opinions.
- **Ship findings in formats stakeholders already read.** If the eng team lives in tickets, research lands in tickets. If leadership reads dashboards, measure happiness on a dashboard.
- **Nine signs of research impact** (from *It's Our Research*) — track whether stakeholders are asking for more research, citing findings in decisions, and changing behavior, not whether a report got praised.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Question → method picker (Validating Product Ideas spine):**
  | Product question | Suggested lean method |
  |---|---|
  | Who are our users? | Customer interviews, diary studies |
  | What do they need? | Contextual inquiry, story-based interviews |
  | Is there a market? | Fake-door tests, landing-page experiments |
  | How do they currently solve this? | Competitive teardowns, observation |
  | Will they buy it? | Concept tests, wizard-of-oz, pre-sales |
  | Can they use it? | Usability testing (5 users, task-based) |
  | Are they happy? | HEART metrics, post-launch interviews |
- **Stakeholder research plan template (It's Our Research):**
  - Decision being supported (named)
  - Stakeholders: decision-maker, influencers, blockers
  - What each stakeholder currently believes
  - What findings would actually change their mind
  - Shared agreement on success criteria before fieldwork starts
- **"Nine signs of impact" scorecard** — run quarterly on the research function to audit whether insights are actually landing.
- **Lean research sprint (one week):** Mon — agree on question + stakeholders; Tue–Wed — 5 sessions; Thu — synthesis with stakeholders in the room; Fri — written one-pager with decision-supporting options.

## Where they disagree with others
- Sharon vs. "research as a specialist protected function": he actively pushes research out of the research team and into PM/design hands, unlike some academic and large-org traditions that guard methodological rigor centrally.
- Sharon vs. report-heavy deliverables: he favors whatever format gets the insight used (a Slack message, a ticket, a dashboard), not long PDFs.
- Sharon vs. Torres on cadence framing: Torres emphasizes a continuous weekly rhythm; Sharon is more often organized around discrete product-question sprints. Both are compatible but emphasize different loops.
