# ResearchOps Community

## Why they matter to the Researcher
The ResearchOps Community is the practitioner home for how research *operations* — repositories, templates, participant management, governance, and democratization — actually get done. For the Researcher agent, this is the reference for the non-glamorous layer: how agent-produced findings get stored, tagged, indexed, and surfaced so they don't rot in individual Notion pages. Their "Eight Pillars" model is a complete operating-system for a research function, and their community-built templates map directly onto the Notion research-repository schema the Researcher should maintain. The community's ethos — democratize research, share templates openly, document everything — aligns with the agent's "present options, don't decide" mandate: a good repository surfaces prior findings to the whole team so decisions are informed by context, not rediscovery.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **The Eight Pillars.** Participants, Governance, Tools & infrastructure, Knowledge management, Competency, Organizational context, Scope, and Socialization. Every ReOps question lives in one of these pillars.
- **Operations is the substrate, not the output.** Good ops makes good research possible; it is not the research itself. But bad ops silently degrades every study.
- **Templates are the unit of shareable knowledge.** Research plans, consent forms, recruiting screeners, synthesis canvases — all live as open templates the community iterates on.
- **Research repositories are living systems, not archives.** The goal is a searchable, taggable, re-findable record that serves future decisions, not a graveyard of PDFs.
- **Democratize with guardrails.** Making research everyone's job works only if there's a shared baseline of quality (templates, training, review) — otherwise you get noise.
- **Community-sourced knowledge.** The Eight Pillars were built from a 300-response survey and 33 workshops — the community itself is the research subject.

- **Research repository schema (Notion database fields):**
  - Title, Research question, Method, Date, Researcher, Stakeholders, Participants (linked), Tags (themes, products, segments), Status (in-progress / synthesized / archived), Key findings (3 bullets), Linked decisions, Raw artifacts (transcripts, recordings), Confidence, Source URLs
- **Consent / recruiting screener templates** — pull from the ResearchOps GitHub and adapt; don't rewrite from scratch.
- **Findings tagging taxonomy** — start with: theme, product area, user segment, severity, confidence. Add sparingly; every new tag is a maintenance cost.
- **Socialization ritual:** weekly "insight of the week" Slack message + monthly repo tour; otherwise the repository silently dies.

## Where they disagree with others
- ReOps community vs. the "lone researcher" tradition: ReOps insists that scaling research is a systems problem (participants, tooling, governance), not a talent problem. Hiring more researchers without ops investment hits a ceiling fast.
- ReOps vs. Tomer Sharon's democratization framing: both want more people doing research, but ReOps emphasizes the infrastructure that makes democratization safe (templates, governance, review), whereas Sharon emphasizes the mindset shift and stakeholder co-authorship. Complementary.
- ReOps vs. secondary-research shops (Gartner, CB Insights): ReOps is entirely about first-party research operations at a single organization; it has little to say about market-scale data collection.
