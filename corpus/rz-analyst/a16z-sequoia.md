# a16z (Andreessen Horowitz) + Sequoia Capital

## Why they matter to the Analyst routine
The two firms publish more substantive tech-market analysis than any other source — and unlike incumbent analyst firms (Gartner / Forrester), VCs are placing real bets on their analysis, which keeps the work honest. **a16z** publishes "state of" reports for AI, consumer, fintech, biotech, and gaming each year, plus topic deep-dives (Marketplace 100, Open Source AI Index, Consumer Strikes Back). **Sequoia** publishes founder memos, the "Mortar and Pestle" framework for go-to-market, and pieces like "Decoding AI's Growth Curve." For an Analyst routine doing market sizing, opportunity assessment, or competitive positioning in tech-adjacent categories, these are the venture-grade primary sources that supplement (and often supersede) traditional analyst reports.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Markets are large or they aren't.** VCs are obsessed with TAM size because returns require it. Use a16z/Sequoia framing on TAM (bottom-up sizing, comparison to analogous categories) rather than Gartner's top-down sizing as a default.
- **Defensibility is the question.** Andreessen's "What is Defensibility?" framework: brand, network effects, scale economics, embedding (switching costs), regulatory. Every opportunity brief should answer: "If this works, what stops a competitor from copying us?"
- **Distribution beats product, eventually.** "Distribution is hard. Product is hard. But distribution is harder." — paraphrasing many a16z partners. New products without a distribution thesis lose to lesser products with distribution.
- **The "10x better" rule.** A new product needs to be 10x better than the incumbent on the dimension that matters to the buyer to overcome switching cost. 2x is not enough; "feature parity plus AI" is rarely enough.
- **Founder-led companies execute differently.** Sequoia's investment thesis weights founder-fit heavily. Analyst briefs should distinguish founder-led companies (faster, more risk-tolerant, longer time horizons) from executive-run companies (more predictable, slower to pivot).
- **AI changes the math on every old assumption.** Both firms have published extensively on which assumptions about software economics, distribution, and competitive moats break in the AI era. Default to "this assumption may not hold anymore" when the analysis touches AI.
- **The GenAI value chain has multiple layers.** Sequoia's stack model: infrastructure → foundation models → developer tools → applications. Different layers have different defensibility and different unit economics.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **a16z Defensibility Audit** for any opportunity brief or competitive matrix:
  - **Brand** — does the company own a category-defining name? (Google = search, Stripe = payments)
  - **Network effects** — does the product get better as more people use it? Same-side or cross-side?
  - **Scale economics** — does serving the marginal customer get cheaper at scale?
  - **Embedding (switching cost)** — how integrated is the product into the customer's workflow / data / process?
  - **Regulatory** — are there legal/regulatory barriers that protect the position?
  - Score each as low/medium/high; high in 2+ is a strong moat
- **Sequoia GenAI value-chain framing** when the opportunity touches AI:
  - **Infrastructure** layer (chips, data centers, networking) — capital-intensive, oligopolistic, very high barrier
  - **Foundation models** layer — increasingly competitive among well-funded labs; defensibility through data, compute, and talent
  - **Developer tools** layer — RAG, agents, evals, orchestration — fast-moving, fragmented, opportunity-rich
  - **Applications** layer — verticalized for specific industries; defensibility through workflow integration and data network effects
- **a16z 10x test** for any product positioning analysis:
  - On the dimension the buyer cares about most, is this product 10x better than the incumbent?
  - If 2–3x: insufficient; will lose to incumbent inertia
  - If 10x+: sufficient to overcome switching cost
- **TAM sizing from a16z's playbook** (bottom-up rather than top-down):
  - Number of buyers in target segment × ARPU at target price = TAM
  - Cross-check with: analogous category's actual revenue in same segment × growth multiplier for new technology
  - Avoid Gartner-style "$XB market by 2030" — too top-down to be useful for opportunity assessment
- **The "founder-led vs. exec-run" classifier** for any competitive analysis:
  - Is the company still founder-led? Founders make different bets than hired execs.
  - Has the founder's vision shifted recently (board pressure, IPO prep)?

## Where they disagree with others
- a16z vs. Sequoia on AI hype cycle: a16z has been more AI-bullish in public framing; Sequoia has been more cautious about valuations and timing. Routine should be aware both exist and not treat either as definitive.
- VC analysis vs. academic strategy (Porter, Christensen): VCs are forward-looking and bet-driven; academics are retrospective and theory-driven. VCs may be early/wrong; academics may be late/right. Triangulate.
- a16z vs. Stratechery: a16z is invested in companies it analyzes (disclosure built in but bias is real); Stratechery is independent. For opportunity assessment of a16z portfolio companies, weight Stratechery's view more heavily.
- VC TAM estimates vs. Gartner/Forrester: VCs systematically over-estimate TAM (it's part of the pitch); analyst firms can under-estimate emerging categories (they trail innovation). Triangulate; don't trust either alone.
