# Clay Christensen

## Why they matter to the Analyst routine
Christensen contributed two ideas that the Analyst routine reaches for constantly. **Disruptive innovation** (1997) is the explanation for why successful incumbents lose to entrants who initially offered "worse" products — disruptors enter at the low end (or in non-consumption), iterate up-market, and eventually displace the incumbent who was "rationally" focused on its best customers. Every analyst brief on a startup-vs-incumbent dynamic uses this lens. **Jobs-to-be-Done** (JTBD) reframes buyer analysis: customers don't buy products, they "hire" them to make progress in a specific situation. JTBD is the antidote to demographic-segmentation analysis — it explains *why* a buyer chooses one option over another (or over non-consumption) in terms of the underlying job, and it's the lens for opportunity briefs and competitive matrices that compete across category boundaries.

## Signature works & primary sources
- *The Innovator's Dilemma: When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail* (Christensen, 1997) — the foundational text on disruptive innovation theory.
- *The Innovator's Solution* (Christensen + Raynor, 2003) — how to apply disruption theory to building new businesses.
- *Competing Against Luck: The Story of Innovation and Customer Choice* (Christensen + Hall, 2016) — the canonical JTBD treatment.
- "Marketing Malpractice: The Cause and the Cure" (HBR, 2005) — the milkshake JTBD case study; the most accessible intro to JTBD.
- *The Prosperity Paradox* (2019) — how innovation creates new market consumption.
- Christensen Institute — https://www.christenseninstitute.org/ — ongoing research on disruption, education, healthcare.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Disruption is a process, not an event.** Disruptors enter at the low end (or in non-consumption) where incumbents don't compete, iterate to absorb mainstream needs, then displace. Identifying disruption requires watching the bottom of the market, not the top.
- **The incumbent's customers are the trap.** Incumbents lose because they listen rationally to their best customers, who don't want the disruptor's initial offering. Doing the right thing for current customers and getting disrupted are not mutually exclusive — they're often the same.
- **Jobs are the unit of analysis, not customers or products.** Customers don't buy products — they hire products to do jobs. The same person hires different products for different jobs; the same product is hired by very different people for the same job.
- **Functional, emotional, and social dimensions of a job all matter.** A milkshake is hired (functionally) to satisfy hunger on a long commute, (emotionally) to provide a small treat in a tedious day, and (socially) to feel like a parent who's responsibly indulgent. Strategy that addresses only the functional dimension misses 2/3 of the value.
- **Hiring requires firing.** When a customer hires your product, they fire something else. The "something else" they fire is your real competition — often not what your category analysis suggests.
- **Non-consumption is the biggest opportunity.** Markets where the alternative is "do nothing" or "use a hacky workaround" are bigger than markets where everyone already buys a category solution. Look for non-consumption in opportunity briefs.
- **Sustaining innovation is fine, just don't confuse it with disruption.** Most innovation is sustaining — incremental improvement to existing products for existing customers. That's value-creating and competitive. It's just not the same as disruption.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Disruption assessment** for any competitor or new entrant:
  1. Where did they enter? (low end / new market / mainstream)
  2. Which customers did they target initially? (overshot incumbents' customers / underserved / non-consumers)
  3. Trajectory: are they improving toward the incumbent's mainstream, or staying in their initial segment?
  4. Incumbent's response: are they rationally ignoring (classic dilemma), or attempting to compete?
  5. Time horizon: low-end disruption typically takes 5–10 years to mature; new-market disruption can be faster
- **Job statement template** (the JTBD canonical form):
  - "When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."
  - Example: "When I'm rushing through the morning commute, I want a treat that's quick and one-handed, so I can feel like the day started okay."
  - Use this in personas (paired with `rz-ux-researcher`'s persona skill) and opportunity briefs
- **The "what got fired?" question** for any new product or feature:
  - List the things customers stop doing when they adopt your offering. That's your real competitive set, regardless of category labels.
- **Three-dimension job analysis:**
  - Functional: what task / outcome does the job produce?
  - Emotional: how does the customer want to feel during / after?
  - Social: how does the customer want to be perceived by others?
  - A complete competitive matrix scores competitors on all three.
- **Non-consumption opportunity scan:**
  - Identify customers who currently don't buy any product in your category — what do they do instead?
  - Identify situations where existing customers don't use the category product — what's the workaround?
  - These are your candidate disruption beachheads (or your candidate market expansion beachheads, if you're the incumbent).

## Where they disagree with others
- Christensen vs. Porter: Christensen's disruption theory implicitly criticizes Porter's emphasis on defending position — disruptors win precisely by NOT competing where the incumbent's defenses are strong. Use both: Porter for the snapshot of the current battle, Christensen for the trajectory of who's about to enter.
- Christensen vs. critics of disruption theory (Jill Lepore's 2014 *New Yorker* piece): Lepore argued the theory is over-applied and many of Christensen's case studies don't fit the model on closer examination. Routine's stance: use disruption as a useful lens, not a deterministic prediction. Don't over-claim.
- Christensen vs. Lean Startup / customer-development advocates (Steve Blank, Eric Ries): both school argue for testing demand. Christensen's contribution is the *theoretical structure* (jobs, non-consumption, low-end entry) that explains *why* tests succeed or fail. Complementary.
- JTBD vs. demographic personas: JTBD argues demographic segmentation (35-year-old urban professionals) is a poor predictor of behavior compared to job-based segmentation (people on a 30-min commute who haven't eaten breakfast). Both useful; JTBD is more often correlated with purchase decisions.
