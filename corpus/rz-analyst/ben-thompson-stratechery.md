# Ben Thompson (Stratechery)

## Why they matter to the Analyst routine
Thompson is the most-read tech-strategy writer working today, and his frameworks are the working vocabulary the Analyst routine uses for tech-specific competitive analysis. His **Aggregation Theory** (2015, refined since) reframed how to think about platform competition in the internet era — the dynamic isn't owning supply or distribution, it's owning the user relationship and aggregating demand. His **Conservation of Attractive Profits** lens explains why profits move along value chains as architectures shift (modular vs. integrated). His weekly **Stratechery** updates are real-time strategic analysis of every tech competitive event from the moment it happens, with a level of insight that incumbents and entrants both read religiously. For Riché's tech portfolio, Thompson is the closest thing to a continuous research feed.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Aggregation Theory:** in markets with zero marginal cost (digital), zero distribution cost (internet), and zero search cost (search engines), aggregators that own the *user relationship* capture disproportionate value. Suppliers commoditize; aggregators win. Examples: Google in search, Facebook in social, Netflix in streaming, Amazon in e-commerce.
- **Conservation of Attractive Profits:** profits don't disappear when an architecture shifts; they move. When integrated stacks unbundle (e.g., PC vs. Mac), profits move to the modular layer with the highest scarcity. When modular stacks integrate (e.g., iPhone), profits move back. Knowing where profits will land predicts where competitive intensity will be.
- **The internet's three impacts:** zero marginal distribution cost, zero marginal transaction cost, zero search cost. Each kills a category of intermediary that existed because one of those costs was non-zero. Strategy in tech requires tracking which intermediaries are next.
- **Trust the customer relationship.** In the aggregation era, the company that owns the customer can, over time, capture or commoditize anything upstream. Therefore: build for direct customer relationships, even at the cost of channel-partner conflict in the short term.
- **Modularization happens slowly, then suddenly.** A market that has been integrated for years can unbundle in months once a new aggregator emerges (e.g., banking → fintech). Watch for the unbundling moment.
- **Differentiation is operational, not feature-based.** In commoditized markets, differentiation comes from supply chains, customer relationships, or data network effects — not from features that can be copied in a quarter.
- **Most analyst takes are wrong because they ignore power dynamics.** A "rational" market analysis that ignores who has the customer relationship will mis-predict outcomes. Power asymmetry is the dominant variable.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Aggregation analysis** for any market the routine evaluates:
  - Who currently aggregates demand (owns the customer relationship)?
  - What suppliers does that aggregator commoditize (or could commoditize)?
  - Where in the value chain do profits currently sit?
  - What would change if the aggregator integrated upstream or a supplier integrated downstream?
- **Internet impact scan** when assessing whether a category is about to be disrupted:
  - Is distribution still expensive in this category? If yes — vulnerable to internet distribution.
  - Is transacting still expensive? If yes — vulnerable to platform-mediated transactions.
  - Is searching still expensive? If yes — vulnerable to a recommendation/search aggregator.
- **Architecture-shift detector:** for any tech competitive analysis, ask:
  - Is the current architecture modular or integrated?
  - What's the bundling/unbundling pressure?
  - If architecture shifts in either direction, where do profits move?
- **Power audit** for any competitor:
  - Who owns the customer relationship?
  - Who owns the supply (data, content, inventory, models)?
  - Who owns the distribution channel?
  - The asymmetric layer is the moat (or the vulnerability).
- **The "what would Ben say?" cross-check:** for any opportunity brief in the tech space, search Stratechery archives for prior coverage of the category. Thompson has likely already written something within the last 5 years that frames the dynamics.

## Where they disagree with others
- Thompson vs. classic Porter: Porter's Five Forces ignore the unique dynamics of zero-marginal-cost digital markets. Thompson's Aggregation Theory is the necessary update for tech industries. Both useful.
- Thompson vs. Christensen on disruption: Thompson argues classical disruption theory under-predicts how aggregators displace incumbents (because aggregators don't enter at the low end — they enter by owning the customer relationship at the same price point). The mechanism is different.
- Thompson vs. consumer-surplus-focused analysts: Thompson is more interested in *where profits land* than in consumer welfare per se. Other analysts (especially academic) focus on consumer surplus. Both lenses matter; Thompson's is more strategically actionable for businesses.
- Thompson on big tech regulation: Thompson is generally skeptical of antitrust action against aggregators (argues their position is earned via consumer preference). Critics argue this underweights structural anticompetitive effects. Routine's stance: cite Thompson's framework, note the contested take when relevant.
