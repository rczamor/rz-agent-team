# Michael Porter

## Why they matter to the Analyst routine
Porter is the foundational vocabulary for competitive strategy. Every competitive matrix, market analysis, and positioning brief the routine produces traces back to one of three Porter frameworks: **Five Forces** (the structural attractiveness of an industry), **Generic Strategies** (cost leadership / differentiation / focus — the choices a company can make about how to compete), and the **Value Chain** (the activities that create cost or differentiation advantages). Porter's 1996 HBR article "What is Strategy?" is the most-quoted definition of strategy in modern business writing — strategy is making *trade-offs*, doing things differently from rivals, with **fit** between activities. The routine cites Porter when grounding why a competitor is positioned where they are or why an opportunity has structural barriers.

## Signature works & primary sources
- *Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors* (Porter, 1980) — the foundational text on Five Forces and generic strategies.
- *Competitive Advantage: Creating and Sustaining Superior Performance* (Porter, 1985) — the value chain framework.
- "What is Strategy?" (HBR, Nov–Dec 1996) — https://hbr.org/1996/11/what-is-strategy — the most-cited Porter article. Strategy as activity-system fit, not best practices.
- "The Five Competitive Forces That Shape Strategy" (HBR, Jan 2008) — https://hbr.org/2008/01/the-five-competitive-forces-that-shape-strategy — Porter's update to Five Forces with a quarter-century of refinements.
- "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy" (HBR, 1979) — original Five Forces article.
- Institute for Strategy and Competitiveness (Harvard) — https://www.isc.hbs.edu/ — Porter's research home.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Industry structure determines profitability.** The single biggest predictor of whether a company is profitable is the industry it competes in — Five Forces analysis quantifies this. Strategy starts with industry analysis, not with the company.
- **The Five Forces:** rivalry among competitors, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers. Each force shapes profit potential. Stronger forces → harder to make money.
- **Strategy is making trade-offs.** "The essence of strategy is choosing what NOT to do." Companies that try to be all things to all customers — the "stuck in the middle" trap — underperform companies with a clear positional choice.
- **Operational effectiveness ≠ strategy.** Doing the same things faster or better isn't strategy — it's table stakes. Strategy is doing *different* things or doing them in *different ways* that produce a defensible position.
- **Activity-system fit creates moat.** A position is hard to copy when many activities reinforce each other. Competitors can copy one activity easily; replicating an entire activity system is much harder. Look for "fit" when assessing competitor moats.
- **Generic strategies are mutually exclusive at the business-unit level.** A single business unit cannot simultaneously pursue cost leadership and differentiation without losing focus. Conglomerates can host both, but each unit picks one.
- **Substitution is the underestimated force.** Competitors in the named industry are obvious; substitutes from adjacent industries (different solution to the same buyer's job) are where strategic surprises come from.
- **Buyer concentration is asymmetric leverage.** Industries serving few large buyers (enterprise software, defense) have very different dynamics than those serving many small buyers (consumer SaaS). Five Forces forces this distinction.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Five Forces analysis template** (use whenever a competitive matrix needs structural framing):
  - **Rivalry:** number of competitors, growth rate, exit barriers, fixed-cost ratio, differentiation level
  - **New entrants:** capital requirements, regulatory barriers, brand-name advantages, network effects, scale economies
  - **Substitutes:** direct alternatives in adjacent categories; the *job* the buyer is trying to do, with all means of accomplishing it
  - **Supplier power:** supplier concentration, switching costs, threat of forward integration
  - **Buyer power:** buyer concentration, switching costs, price sensitivity, threat of backward integration
  - Score each force low/medium/high; the highest force usually defines the competitive dynamic
- **Generic strategy classifier** for any competitor:
  - Cost leadership (Walmart, Aldi) — lowest delivered cost, broad scope
  - Differentiation (Apple, Tesla) — premium price for unique value, broad scope
  - Cost focus (Costco for warehouse buyers) — lowest cost in a narrow segment
  - Differentiation focus (Patagonia, Notion-for-engineers) — unique value in a narrow segment
  - Stuck-in-the-middle — neither low cost nor differentiated; underperforms structurally
- **Value chain breakdown** for cost or differentiation analysis:
  - Primary activities: inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing/sales, service
  - Support activities: firm infrastructure, HRM, technology development, procurement
  - For each activity, ask: where do we have a cost advantage? a differentiation advantage? a parity gap?
- **The "what activities reinforce each other?" question** when assessing a competitor's defensibility — list the activities the competitor does, then map which ones make the others harder for a copycat. The web of fit is the moat.
- **The "is this strategy or operational effectiveness?" filter:** when reading a competitor's stated strategy, ask whether they're making different choices or just executing well. If they're just executing well, the position is fragile.

## Where they disagree with others
- Porter vs. Christensen: Porter analyzes industry structure as relatively static; Christensen emphasizes how disruption from below reshapes industry structure entirely. Both are true at different time horizons — Porter is shorter-term, Christensen is longer-term.
- Porter vs. Rita McGrath (transient advantage): Porter argues for *sustainable* competitive advantage; McGrath argues advantages are increasingly transient and the strategy is to *cycle through advantages*. Routine's stance: Porter for the snapshot, McGrath for the trajectory.
- Porter vs. blue-ocean strategy (Kim & Mauborgne): blue-ocean argues you can escape competition by creating new market space. Porter argues all markets eventually face the Five Forces; blue oceans get fished. Both useful as inputs to opportunity briefs.
- Porter vs. resource-based view (Barney): RBV starts from internal resources and capabilities; Porter starts from external industry structure. Modern strategy synthesizes both.
