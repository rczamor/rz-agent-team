# Gartner & Forrester

## Why they matter to the Analyst routine
Gartner and Forrester are the two incumbent analyst firms that define the vocabulary Riché's enterprise buyers use to evaluate vendors. **Gartner Magic Quadrants** plot vendors on two axes (Ability to Execute × Completeness of Vision) into 4 quadrants (Leaders, Challengers, Visionaries, Niche Players). **Hype Cycles** track the trajectory of emerging technologies through Innovation Trigger → Peak of Inflated Expectations → Trough of Disillusionment → Slope of Enlightenment → Plateau of Productivity. **Forrester Wave** reports score vendors across weighted criteria with a more transparent methodology than Magic Quadrants. **Total Economic Impact (TEI)** studies are vendor-commissioned ROI analyses that buyers cite. For any Analyst brief on a category where enterprise buyers participate, knowing the Gartner/Forrester landscape is the cost of entry — even when their conclusions are wrong, they're what the buyers reference.

## Signature works & primary sources
**Gartner:**
- gartner.com (mostly paywalled; abstracts free)
- Magic Quadrant reports — vendor positioning across categories (issued 1–2x per year per category)
- Hype Cycle reports — trajectory of emerging technologies (annual, by domain)
- Critical Capabilities reports — deeper feature-by-feature scoring than Magic Quadrants
- Predicts (annual) — Gartner's industry forecasts
- IT Glossary (free) — https://www.gartner.com/en/information-technology/glossary
- Gartner Peer Insights (free) — verified user reviews of enterprise software
- Gartner Symposium / IT Symposium — annual flagship conference

**Forrester:**
- forrester.com (paywalled)
- Wave reports — vendor positioning with explicit weighted criteria (more transparent than Magic Quadrants)
- Total Economic Impact (TEI) studies — vendor-commissioned ROI analyses
- Forrester Tech Tide reports — emerging-tech adoption framework
- Forrester Now Tech reports — broader landscape view across vendor categories
- Customer Experience (CX) Index — annual brand-experience benchmark
- Forrester Decisions service — analyst access for paying clients

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **The reports define buyer vocabulary.** Buyers ask for "Leaders quadrant" vendors, "Wave Strong Performers," and "Hype Cycle Plateau of Productivity" technologies. Talking to enterprise buyers without knowing this vocabulary marks you as an outsider.
- **Methodology matters more than conclusions.** Magic Quadrants are notoriously gamed (vendors invest heavily to move within them). Trust the methodology — what's measured, how — more than the placement.
- **The Hype Cycle is descriptively accurate but not predictive.** Most technologies that reach the Trough of Disillusionment never recover; Gartner positions them as if the Slope of Enlightenment is inevitable. Use the framework as a lens, not a forecast.
- **TEI studies are biased but useful as starting points.** Always vendor-commissioned, but they reveal what ROI metrics matter to buyers and provide reference numbers. Take with sodium chloride.
- **Forrester is more methodologically transparent.** Wave reports show the weighted criteria; Magic Quadrants don't. For a defensible analysis, Forrester is easier to cite.
- **Both firms lag emerging categories.** Crypto, GenAI applications, AI-native dev tools — Gartner and Forrester typically pick up coverage 1–2 years after VCs are funding the space. They're not where you spot inflection points; they're where you find post-hoc consensus.
- **Peer reviews (Gartner Peer Insights, G2, Capterra) often beat the analyst takes.** For functional comparison, end-user reviews are more honest than the formal reports. Use both.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Gartner Magic Quadrant decoder** when reading a competitor's MQ position:
  - Leaders (top-right): high execution + high vision; usually large, established
  - Challengers (top-left): high execution + lower vision; often legacy with strong customer base
  - Visionaries (bottom-right): high vision + lower execution; often startups with strong product but limited scale
  - Niche Players (bottom-left): focused on specific segments; often the right answer for narrow use cases
  - Don't conflate "Leader" with "best fit for your use case"
- **Hype Cycle position-check** for any emerging-tech opportunity brief:
  - Innovation Trigger: too early; product-market fit unclear
  - Peak of Inflated Expectations: hype-driven valuations; bandwagon pressure; many will fail
  - Trough of Disillusionment: most promising window for differentiated entry; survivors are stronger
  - Slope of Enlightenment: real adoption beginning; reference customers exist
  - Plateau of Productivity: mainstream adoption; competitive intensity high
- **TEI study extraction template** when reading a Forrester TEI:
  - What's the claimed payback period? Verify with peer reviews.
  - What benefits are quantified? Are they direct (cost savings) or indirect (productivity)?
  - What's the discount rate assumed? (TEIs often use unrealistic discount rates)
  - What's NOT included? (Implementation, integration, training, opportunity cost)
- **Cross-reference cheatsheet** for any vendor evaluation:
  - Pull the most recent Magic Quadrant if it exists
  - Pull the most recent Forrester Wave for the same category
  - Pull G2/Capterra reviews for the named vendors
  - Pull Gartner Peer Insights if accessible
  - Compare: where do they agree? where do they disagree? Disagreement is signal.
- **Citation pattern** for any Analyst brief that mentions a category:
  - "Gartner positions [Vendor] as a [Quadrant] in the [Year] [Category] Magic Quadrant"
  - "Forrester's [Year] Wave for [Category] rates [Vendor] as a [tier]"
  - "Per Gartner's [Year] Hype Cycle for [Domain], [Technology] is at the [stage]"

## Where they disagree with others
- Gartner/Forrester vs. a16z/Sequoia: incumbent analysts are descriptive (where the market is); VCs are predictive (where the market is going). Use both: incumbent analysts for current state, VCs for forward-looking opportunity briefs.
- Gartner vs. Forrester: Magic Quadrants are more famous and more politically gamed; Wave reports are more methodologically rigorous. For functional analysis, Forrester is often cleaner.
- Gartner Magic Quadrant vs. peer reviews: MQ position and end-user satisfaction frequently diverge. Peer reviews surface usability and support issues that MQ analysts often don't see.
- Hype Cycle vs. Christensen on disruption: Hype Cycle assumes a deterministic trajectory (peak → trough → plateau); Christensen's disruption framework predicts non-linear shifts. Both are useful; neither is the full story.
