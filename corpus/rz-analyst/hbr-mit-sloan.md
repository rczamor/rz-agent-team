# Harvard Business Review & MIT Sloan Management Review

## Why they matter to the Analyst routine
HBR and MIT SMR are the two long-running publications of business strategy and management research. They're the citation source the Analyst routine reaches for when grounding a methodology — not because they predict markets but because they're where the durable strategic frameworks were first published and continue to be refined. Porter's "What is Strategy?" appeared in HBR. Christensen's disruption articles appeared in HBR. McGrath's transient-advantage essay appeared in HBR. Joan Magretta's analysis of Porter, Roger Martin's strategy-as-cascade-of-choices, John Doerr on OKRs, Geoffrey Moore on technology adoption — the canonical articles cited in every business-school strategy course are mostly HBR. **MIT Sloan Management Review** runs at a slightly more academic register and is stronger on data-driven research, AI/digital transformation, and organizational design. Together they're the academic-rigor backstop for any opportunity brief that needs a defensible framework citation.

## Signature works & primary sources
**HBR:**
- hbr.org — https://hbr.org — most articles paywalled; abstracts free; ~5 free articles per month
- HBR Classics — the most-cited articles distilled (Porter's Five Forces, Drucker's "Theory of the Business," Christensen's "Marketing Malpractice," Moore's "Crossing the Chasm," etc.)
- HBR's *On Point* and *Must Reads* anthologies — themed collections (strategy, innovation, AI, leadership)
- HBR podcasts: *HBR IdeaCast*, *Cold Call* (case studies), *Coaching Real Leaders*
- HBR Subscriber Exclusive: full archive back to 1922

**MIT Sloan Management Review:**
- sloanreview.mit.edu — https://sloanreview.mit.edu — partial paywall, free articles each month
- *Frontiers* (research-led articles on emerging topics)
- *Big Idea Initiatives* — themed series (AI & Business Strategy, Future of Work, Sustainability)
- "Becoming a Data-Driven Organization" — long-running research thread
- "AI & Business Strategy" — annual industry-survey research with BCG GAMMA
- BCG-MIT Sloan AI report (annual) — survey-based research on enterprise AI adoption

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Strategy frameworks have lineages.** Most popular frameworks build on prior frameworks published in HBR/SMR. Citing the canonical version (rather than a later derivative) is what credible analysts do. The routine should default to HBR/SMR for first-principles citations.
- **Case studies are the dominant teaching format.** HBR Cases (~$10 each, often free in academic databases) walk through company decisions in detail and identify the strategic choices made. Case studies are higher-fidelity than analyst reports for understanding *how* a strategy plays out.
- **MIT SMR runs at a higher data-rigor bar.** SMR articles often include survey data, regression analysis, or longitudinal studies. HBR articles are often more practitioner-narrative. Both useful; SMR is more defensible for quantitative claims.
- **The "executive summary" tax.** HBR/SMR articles typically open with the practitioner thesis (1–2 paragraphs) before diving into the academic argument. Read the opening paragraphs first; commit to the full read only if the thesis warrants it.
- **The 10-year half-life.** Most HBR/SMR articles age well; some don't. Articles older than 10 years are usually still cited but should be cross-checked for whether their underlying assumptions still hold. Particularly true for tech-industry pieces.
- **Authors matter as much as articles.** Tracking which HBR/SMR authors are still publishing, what they're researching now, and whether their thinking has evolved is more valuable than memorizing specific articles. Build an author-watching list (Porter, Christensen, McGrath, Govindarajan, Iansiti, Brynjolfsson, Edmondson, etc.).

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Citation hierarchy** for the Analyst routine when grounding a framework:
  1. The original HBR/SMR article that introduced the framework (gold standard — most rigorous)
  2. The author's book elaborating the framework (deeper context, examples)
  3. A subsequent HBR/SMR article applying or refining the framework (shows evolution)
  4. A practitioner blog or VC firm's interpretation (useful for current relevance, weaker for academic credibility)
- **HBR-style argument structure** for opportunity briefs and market analyses:
  - Open with the contrarian thesis (1 paragraph): what most people believe + why it's wrong
  - Establish the new framework or analysis (1–2 paragraphs): the lens through which the rest of the argument holds
  - Apply to specific examples (3–5 paragraphs): case studies, data, or specific market dynamics
  - Close with implications for the reader (1 paragraph): what to do differently given the analysis
  - The Analyst routine's opportunity briefs should mirror this structure — opens with the thesis, establishes the lens, applies to evidence, closes with implications
- **Annual reading list** (suggested HBR/SMR articles to track quarterly):
  - HBR's *Top 10 Articles of the Year* (December issue)
  - SMR's *Best Articles of the Year* (winter issue)
  - HBR's "What Works" series (specific tactics)
  - BCG-MIT SMR AI Report (annual, free with registration)
  - HBR's IdeaCast podcast for new article discovery
- **Author-watching list** (worth tracking new publications from):
  - Strategy: Roger Martin, Marco Iansiti, Karim Lakhani, Felix Oberholzer-Gee, Rita McGrath
  - Innovation: Andrew Binns, Vijay Govindarajan, Scott Anthony
  - Tech & AI: Erik Brynjolfsson, Andrew McAfee, Sinan Aral
  - Org design: Amy Edmondson, Adam Grant
  - Marketing & buyer behavior: Tony Ulwick, Ryan Hamilton

## Where they disagree with others
- HBR/SMR vs. analyst firms (Gartner/Forrester): HBR/SMR are theory-and-research-driven; analyst firms are vendor-and-product-driven. Both useful; HBR/SMR for the strategic frame, analyst firms for the current-state vendor landscape.
- HBR/SMR vs. Stratechery and substack-era writers: HBR/SMR are slower (monthly/quarterly); substack writers are faster but less peer-reviewed. For first-principles framework citations, HBR/SMR; for real-time analysis, Stratechery + substacks.
- HBR vs. SMR on data rigor: SMR articles more often include data and methodology sections; HBR is more often narrative/practitioner. For evidence-based claims, prefer SMR; for accessibility and synthesis, HBR.
- HBR/SMR vs. McKinsey/BCG/Bain insights: consultancies publish their own thought-leadership content (McKinsey Global Institute, BCG Henderson Institute). Often less academically rigorous than HBR/SMR but more current; sometimes more applicable to specific industries.
