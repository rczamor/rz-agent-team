# Rita McGrath

## Why they matter to the Analyst routine
McGrath is the modern voice on what strategy looks like when sustainable competitive advantage is no longer the norm. Her central thesis (*The End of Competitive Advantage*, 2013) is that in fast-moving industries — most tech, anything AI-adjacent — competitive advantages are *transient*: they emerge, get exploited, and erode in years rather than decades. The strategic capability isn't defending a moat; it's *cycling through transient advantages* faster than competitors. Her *Seeing Around Corners* (2019) is the playbook for spotting **strategic inflection points** — the moments when industry structure shifts and the old competitive logic stops working. The Analyst routine cites McGrath when a market is moving fast enough that Porter-style structural analysis alone misses the dynamic, or when an opportunity brief needs a "what's the next inflection?" framing.

## Signature works & primary sources
- *The End of Competitive Advantage: How to Keep Your Strategy Moving as Fast as Your Business* (McGrath, 2013) — the transient-advantage thesis.
- *Seeing Around Corners: How to Spot Inflection Points in Business Before They Happen* (McGrath, 2019) — practical guide to detecting and responding to strategic inflection points.
- *Discovery-Driven Growth* (McGrath + MacMillan, 2009) — how to plan growth ventures under high uncertainty (planned-learning vs. detailed forecasting).

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Competitive advantages are transient by default.** In fast industries, the lifecycle of an advantage is: launch → ramp up → exploit → reconfigure → disengage. The strategic skill is moving through this cycle deliberately, not defending stage 3 indefinitely.
- **Resource allocation must follow the lifecycle.** Investments concentrated on mature advantages (defending the moat) starve emerging advantages (the next moat). McGrath recommends an "options portfolio" of small bets in the launch stage.
- **Strategic inflection points are detectable.** Five categories of warning signs: changes in technology, regulation, customer demographics, lifestyle, and the competitive landscape. Watching the periphery (where weak signals appear) gives years of lead time.
- **Discovery-driven planning beats forecasting under uncertainty.** When the answer is unknown, plan in milestones with explicit assumptions; revise the plan as each assumption is tested. Forecasting works for known industries; discovery-driven planning works for new ones.
- **The "right wrong" answers are valuable.** A failed venture that taught you something about an unknown is worth more than a successful exploitation of a known. The unit of strategic learning is the assumption falsified.
- **Strategy is a portfolio, not a position.** A single position assumes the world is stable. A portfolio of advantages at different lifecycle stages assumes the world is shifting and hedges across futures.
- **Watch the corner before others do.** Most disruptions arrive years after the warning signs were visible. The discipline is taking weak signals seriously when most competitors are still optimizing the old position.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Transient advantage lifecycle scan** for any competitor or any business unit:
  - Stage 1 (Launch): What are they investing in that hasn't shipped yet?
  - Stage 2 (Ramp up): What's growing fast but not yet at scale?
  - Stage 3 (Exploit): What's the cash cow today?
  - Stage 4 (Reconfigure): What are they retiring or refactoring?
  - Stage 5 (Disengage): What are they selling, killing, or quietly winding down?
  - A healthy competitor has assets in stages 1, 2, AND 3. A vulnerable one has everything in stage 3.
- **Inflection-point watch list** (5 categories from *Seeing Around Corners*):
  - Technology shifts: what new technical capability is becoming feasible/cheap?
  - Regulation: what's about to change legally?
  - Demographics: what's the trajectory of the customer base?
  - Lifestyle: how is buyer behavior changing?
  - Competitive landscape: who's entering, who's exiting, who's pivoting?
  - For each category, name the top 1–2 weak signals worth tracking quarterly.
- **Discovery-driven planning template** for any opportunity brief that involves a new market or new offering:
  - State the desired outcome (e.g., "$5M ARR in 18 months in segment X")
  - Reverse-engineer the operations spec needed to produce that outcome
  - List the key assumptions baked into the spec (price, conversion rate, retention, COGS, etc.)
  - Rank assumptions by uncertainty × impact
  - Define cheap tests for the highest-impact uncertain assumptions
  - Plan in milestones: each milestone tests one or more assumptions
- **The "if this is true, then what?" question** for weak signals: when a small but unusual signal appears, ask "if this trend continues, what does the market look like in 5 years?" Then ask "what would we need to do today to be positioned for that?"
- **The "early warning" interview prompt** when interviewing customers about a new offering: "Tell me about the last time you tried to do this and the existing options didn't work. What did you do instead?" The answer often reveals the inflection point happening at the periphery.

## Where they disagree with others
- McGrath vs. Porter: Porter's frameworks assume relatively stable industry structures; McGrath argues structures themselves are increasingly transient. Use both: Porter for the snapshot of *now*; McGrath for the trajectory of *next*.
- McGrath vs. Christensen: complementary — Christensen explains why incumbents fail to see disruption; McGrath provides the framework for spotting inflection points (which include but aren't limited to disruption). McGrath's framework is broader and applies to more than tech-style disruption.
- McGrath vs. lean startup advocates (Eric Ries): McGrath's discovery-driven planning predates Lean Startup by ~15 years and operates at a higher altitude (corporate strategy + new ventures); Lean Startup is the operational version for startups. Compatible.
- McGrath vs. "moat" investors (Buffett, Pat Dorsey): traditional moat investors look for *durable* advantages. McGrath argues durable advantages are increasingly rare. Useful tension: ask of any moat claim, "what's the half-life?"
