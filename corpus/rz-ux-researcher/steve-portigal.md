# Steve Portigal

## Why they matter to the User Researcher routine
Portigal is the canonical reference on interview craft — the moment-by-moment skills that make the difference between an interview that produces useful data and one that produces nothing but reaffirmed assumptions. *Interviewing Users* (1st ed 2013, 2nd ed 2023) is the single most-cited book on UX research interview methodology: how to elicit stories rather than opinions, how to handle silence, how to detect and escape your own confirmation bias mid-interview, how to follow up on small surprising details that reveal the real model behind the surface answers. This routine's `interview-synthesis` skill depends on the input quality of the interviews, and Portigal is the reference for what "good interviewing" looks like.

## Signature works & primary sources
- *Interviewing Users: How to Uncover Compelling Insights* (Rosenfeld Media, 1st ed 2013, 2nd ed 2023) — the canonical book; the 2nd edition adds a decade of refined practice and modern remote-interview techniques.
- *Doorbells, Danger, and Dead Batteries: User Research War Stories* (Rosenfeld Media, 2017) — collection of war stories from practitioners about research that went sideways; teaches by counter-example.
- *Dollars to Donuts* podcast — long-running interview series with research leaders at companies (Slack, Spotify, Etsy, etc.); free.
- portigal.com — blog, talks, workshop materials.
- "What's the Real Question?" workshop materials — how to spend serious time defining the research question before designing the study.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Stories over opinions.** "Tell me about the last time you…" beats "what do you think about…?" every time. Specific past behaviors are reliable; hypothetical preferences are not. The single biggest lever in interview quality.
- **Embrace silence.** Most interviewers fill silence with the next question. Portigal: count to 5. The participant often elaborates after silence in ways they wouldn't have with a direct probe. Silence is a research instrument.
- **Listen for surprises.** When the participant says something you didn't expect, follow it. The pre-written script is a starting point, not a script to read verbatim. The most valuable findings come from following surprises.
- **Your hypotheses are bias risks.** Going into an interview with a strong hypothesis means hearing what confirms it. Treat hypotheses as things to actively test, not defend.
- **Feature pitches are not interviews.** Once you describe what you're building and ask "would you use this?", the data is contaminated by social desirability bias and the interviewer-effect. Save feature reactions for usability tests, not generative interviews.
- **Interview the person, not the role.** A "designer" or "developer" interview will surface generic role-talk. Probing into the specific person's specific work history surfaces their actual mental model.
- **The interview is a designed experience.** Length, environment, opening warm-up, closing — all designed. Bad design produces bad data even with good questions.
- **Calibration matters.** Interviewers who don't review their own recordings drift in habits. Watching your own interviews periodically is the only way to improve.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Story-prompt question bank** (Portigal's recommended openers):
  - "Tell me about the last time you [specific activity]."
  - "Walk me through what happened the most recent time you tried to [task]."
  - "When was the last time something didn't work the way you expected?"
  - "Describe a typical [time period] for you in [context]."
  - Avoid: "Do you ever…?" "Would you…?" "What do you think about…?"
- **The 5-second silence rule:** after the participant finishes a thought, count to 5 in your head before asking the next question. The elaboration after silence is often where the real insight lives. The routine should preserve this in interview-synthesis: tag quotes that came after silence as higher-fidelity.
- **The "what didn't work?" probe:** when a participant describes a successful outcome, follow with "what almost didn't work?" or "what was the hardest part?" — this surfaces the workarounds and pain points that successful narratives hide.
- **The "show me" request:** when a participant describes a process, ask if they can show you (screen-share, demo, walk through the file/tool). What people *do* and what they *say they do* often diverge meaningfully.
- **Interview structure template** (from *Interviewing Users*):
  - Opening (5 min): rapport, context-setting, consent
  - Background (10 min): role, history, current work
  - Story prompt (20 min): "tell me about the last time you…" + probes
  - Specific scenarios (15 min): targeted questions related to the research goal
  - Wrap-up (5 min): "anything I should have asked?" + thank you
  - Total ~55 min; never exceed 60 min in one session
- **Post-interview debrief template** (immediately after every interview):
  - 3 things that surprised me
  - 3 things that confirmed expectations (note as caution: confirmation may be confirmation bias)
  - 1 thing the participant said that I didn't fully understand (follow up later)
  - Hypothesis updates: which hypotheses got stronger? weaker? new ones?

## Where they disagree with others
- Portigal vs. survey-first researchers: Portigal is skeptical of surveys for generative research — they capture attitudes, not behaviors. Use surveys for confirmation at scale, not for understanding what people do.
- Portigal vs. focus-group practitioners: Portigal opposes focus groups for most user research goals — group dynamics produce social-conformity data, not individual insight. Erika Hall agrees with this. The routine should default to one-on-one interviews, not focus groups.
- Portigal vs. user-testing-only camp: usability testing is for evaluating designs; generative interviews are for understanding the world. Both are needed; substituting one for the other produces wrong inputs to design.
- Portigal vs. JTBD-only camp (Bob Moesta): JTBD interviews are highly structured around the timeline of the buying decision; Portigal's approach is more open-ended discovery. Both can produce high-quality data; choose based on the research question (JTBD for purchase-decision research, Portigal for broader behavioral understanding).
