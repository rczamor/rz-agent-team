# Indi Young

## Why they matter to the User Researcher routine
Young is the methodologist behind two ideas the routine relies on. **Mental models** (her 2008 book of the same name) is the practice of mapping how users *think* about a problem space — their conceptual structure, their reasoning sequence, their categorical assumptions — before designing the product's structure. A product whose information architecture matches the user's mental model is intuitive; a product whose structure is the company's org chart isn't. **Listening deeply** (her *Practical Empathy* 2015 and *Time to Listen* 2022) is a specific interview methodology focused on understanding how the participant thinks about a problem — surfacing the inner reasoning, not the stated preferences. This is complementary to Torres's behavior-focused interviews and Portigal's interview craft: Young's contribution is what to listen FOR.

## Signature works & primary sources
- *Mental Models: Aligning Design Strategy with Human Behavior* (Rosenfeld Media, 2008) — the foundational text on the mental-models method; introduces the "mental model diagram" as a research artifact.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Listen for thinking styles, not preferences.** The goal of an interview isn't to learn what someone wants — it's to understand how they reason. The same person reasons very differently about different problems; capturing the *thinking style* in this problem space is what informs design.
- **Mental models map cognitive structure.** A mental-model diagram is a visualization of how participants conceptually organize a problem domain — the categories they reach for, the sequences they expect, the relationships they assume. The product's information architecture should match (or at least not violate) the dominant mental model.
- **Empathy is a research practice, not a feeling.** Empathy in Young's framing isn't sympathy or sentiment; it's the discipline of suspending your own perspective long enough to inhabit someone else's reasoning. This is teachable, practicable, and measurable in interview output quality.
- **Research participants are not "users."** The label "user" reduces a person to their relationship with your product. Better framing: "people doing the things our product touches." This re-framing changes the questions you ask and the patterns you find.
- **Inner thinking precedes behavior.** Behaviors are the visible output; inner reasoning is the cause. Studying behaviors alone (as in pure analytics) misses the cause and produces designs that work on the surface but break when reasoning shifts.
- **Synthesis is the pattern-finding craft.** Young is rigorous about synthesis methodology: re-listening to interviews, surfacing themes from the language participants use, organizing by inner thinking patterns rather than demographic clusters.
- **Inclusion is methodological, not just ethical.** Designing only for the dominant thinking style produces products that fail subordinate or different thinking styles. Recruiting interview participants across thinking styles (not just across demographics) is a methodological commitment.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Mental-model diagram structure** (the routine can produce these as a complement to journey maps for complex flows):
  - Top section: tasks the user undertakes in this problem space (high-level activities, their words)
  - Middle section: cognitive actions / reasoning steps the user takes (how they decide, evaluate, prepare)
  - Bottom section: features/affordances of the product or environment that support each cognitive action (or fail to)
  - Misalignment between top-middle (what users actually do/think) and bottom (what the product supports) is the design opportunity
- **Listening-deeply interview structure:**
  1. Begin with the participant's own framing of the topic (open, not leading)
  2. Probe on inner thinking: "What were you considering when you decided…?"
  3. Surface the cognitive sequence: "What came first — and then what?"
  4. Capture the language: don't paraphrase the participant's terms in the question; reuse them. The vocabulary itself is data.
  5. Reflect back ("What I heard you say was…") to confirm understanding without judgment
  6. End with: "What didn't I ask that I should have?"
- **Synthesis discipline** (post-interview):
  - Transcribe verbatim; do NOT paraphrase
  - Mark every distinct cognitive action the participant described
  - Cluster cognitive actions across all interviews (not by participant — by action type)
  - Look for the dominant patterns AND the outliers — outliers often reveal underserved thinking styles
- **Thinking-style sampling check** when planning interview recruitment:
  - Have you sampled across at least 3 different thinking styles in the relevant problem space?
  - Are participants varied on dimensions that predict reasoning differences (expertise, trust, comfort with ambiguity, decision-making approach)?
  - Recruiting for demographic variety alone often produces participants with similar thinking styles, missing genuine cognitive diversity.
- **The "no preference questions" rule:** Young opposes asking participants what they want or prefer. Instead: ask what they did, considered, decided, and why. Stated preferences are usually rationalizations of unstated reasoning; the unstated reasoning is the actual research target.

## Where they disagree with others
- Young vs. Torres (continuous discovery): Torres focuses on opportunities and assumption tests; Young focuses on the cognitive structure underlying behavior. Compatible — Torres produces the discovery cadence, Young provides the depth methodology for what gets explored within each interview.
- Young vs. Goodwin (personas): Young's mental-models method is sometimes positioned as an alternative to personas; Young herself sees them as complementary (different lenses on the same data). Personas show *who* the people are; mental models show *how* they think.
- Young vs. JTBD interviewers (Bob Moesta): JTBD interviews follow a tight timeline-of-purchase structure. Young's listening method is more open-ended. Both can produce excellent data; JTBD better for purchase-decision research, listening-deeply better for understanding ongoing reasoning in a domain.
- Young vs. analytics-driven product teams: Young's framing — that inner thinking precedes behavior — is a pointed critique of analytics-only product methodologies. Behavior data tells you what; reasoning research tells you why. Both are needed; behavior alone is insufficient.
