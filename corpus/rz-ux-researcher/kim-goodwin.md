# Kim Goodwin

## Why they matter to the User Researcher routine
Goodwin literally wrote the book on goal-directed design and rigorous persona construction. *Designing for the Digital Age: How to Create Human-Centered Products and Services* (2009) remains the most thorough reference on the persona method — the version that's defensible, evidence-based, and actually informs design decisions, as opposed to the shallow, demographics-driven personas most teams ship and then ignore. Goodwin's lineage runs through Cooper (Alan Cooper's *About Face*, where the modern persona method was popularized) and forward to her workshops at Rosenfeld and her client work. For this routine's `persona` skill, Goodwin is the source for the persona structure — particularly the rigor on goals (the *why* behind behavior), behavioral variables, and the discipline of building personas from observed behavior rather than from imagination.

## Signature works & primary sources
- *Designing for the Digital Age: How to Create Human-Centered Products and Services* (Wiley, 2009) — the definitive treatment of goal-directed design and persona construction; ~700 pages, methodologically rigorous.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
- **Goals, not features, drive behavior.** A persona is built around what the user is fundamentally trying to accomplish (goals) — not their demographics, not their tool preferences. Goals are stable; behaviors and tools are not. Designs aimed at features go out of date; designs aimed at goals don't.
- **Three goal types:** experience goals (how the user wants to feel during the interaction), end goals (what they're trying to accomplish), life goals (deeper aspirations the product touches). All three matter; experience and life goals are the ones most teams ignore.
- **Behavioral variables, not demographics.** Personas should differ on behavioral dimensions (frequency, expertise, attitude, decision-making style) — not on age, gender, or job title. Behavioral variables predict design needs; demographics correlate weakly.
- **Personas are derived from research, not invented.** A persona unconnected to actual interviews and observations is fiction. The Goodwin method explicitly: 5–10 interviews per persona type, transcribed, then variables extracted, clusters identified, archetypes constructed.
- **Three to five personas per product, not more.** Diluting design across 12 personas means designing for none of them. The discipline is force-ranking and prioritizing.
- **The primary persona is whom you design for first.** Secondary personas are accommodated, not optimized for. Negative personas (who you explicitly DON'T design for) are sometimes useful to make scope clear.
- **Personas without scenarios don't drive design.** A persona alone is just a profile. Scenarios (a persona using the product to accomplish a goal in a specific context) are what design teams actually build against.
- **Anti-personas reduce scope creep.** Naming the personas you are NOT designing for is as important as naming the ones you are. Without anti-personas, every stakeholder request seems valid.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse
- **Goodwin's persona structure** (the routine's `persona` skill follows this):
  - Name + photo (composite, not real person)
  - Demographics: only what's directly relevant (avoid age/gender unless behavior-correlated)
  - **Goals** (3 types): experience, end, life — each with 1–3 specific goals
  - **Behaviors**: typical day, frequency of relevant tasks, current tools, workarounds
  - **Mental model**: how they conceptualize the problem space (often very different from designers' model)
  - **Pain points**: top 3, with evidence (interview quote or observation)
  - **Anti-persona pairing**: who this persona is NOT (the segment to deprioritize)
- **Behavioral variable identification** (the analysis step before persona construction):
  - List 8–12 behavioral dimensions relevant to the product
  - Plot interview participants on each dimension (a continuum, not categories)
  - Look for clusters — participants who fall in similar zones across multiple dimensions
  - Each cluster becomes a candidate persona
- **Scenario template** to pair with each persona:
  - Context: where, when, what just happened before this scenario
  - Goal: what the persona is trying to accomplish
  - Steps: what they would do, in their own model — not in the product's model
  - Decision points: where the persona has to choose between options
  - Failure modes: what would cause them to abandon
  - The routine's `journey-map` skill is essentially scenarios at the flow level
- **The "what would change my recommendation?" persona check:** before publishing a persona, ask "what evidence would falsify this persona type?" If you can't name any, the persona may be overfitted to a single interview or to designer assumptions.
- **Persona maintenance cadence:** review personas every 6–12 months. Behaviors evolve; tools evolve. Personas that haven't been updated in 2+ years are likely stale.

## Where they disagree with others
- Goodwin/Cooper personas vs. lean personas (proto-personas, Marty Cagan-style): proto-personas are quickly drafted from team assumptions, no research. Goodwin opposes this — argues proto-personas often perpetuate the very assumptions you should be testing. Routine's stance: research-grounded personas always; proto-personas only as explicit hypothesis statements to test.
- Goodwin vs. JTBD-only camp (Christensen, Moesta): JTBD argues personas are less useful than job statements because the same person hires different products for different jobs. Goodwin agrees jobs matter (her experience/end/life goals frame is JTBD-adjacent) but argues persona archetypes are still useful for design alignment when multiple stakeholders are making decisions.
- Goodwin vs. demographics-driven personas (most marketing personas): Goodwin's whole career is an argument against demographic personas. Behaviors > demographics, every time.
- Goodwin vs. data-only camp ("just use analytics"): Goodwin argues analytics describe what users do but not why; personas (and the qualitative research behind them) explain the why. Both are needed.
