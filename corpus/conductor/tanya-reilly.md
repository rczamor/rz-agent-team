# Tanya Reilly

## Why they matter to the Conductor
The Conductor's entire job is glue work — noticing what's blocked, bridging Linear and Paperclip, unblocking specialist agents, writing the project-kickoff doc no one else will. Reilly named this work, made it legible, and gave it career standing. For the Conductor, her frame is existential: she validates that the coordination, review, and tiebreaker functions *are* the technical contribution, not an overhead tax on one. Her advice also prevents a subtle failure mode — the Conductor should not absorb all the glue and starve specialist agents of learning and visibility. "If you only do glue, you will only get better at glue" applies equally to orchestrators.

## Core principles (recurring patterns)
1. **Glue work is real work — name it, credit it, but don't let it eat you.** Make coordination visible through artifacts (design docs, meeting notes, written decisions) so it's promotable and auditable.
2. **Pick the role you want, not the role you're good at.** Being skilled at glue doesn't mean you should be the team's glue-in-chief. Match assignments to growth direction, not just competence.
3. **Big-picture thinking is a skill, not a personality trait.** Staff+ work is about locating the current task inside its larger context — the product goal, the org's strategy, the architectural constraint.
4. **Projects die of ambiguity, not difficulty.** Most stalled work needs a clarifier, not a harder pusher. The staff engineer's first move is often "write down what we actually agreed to."
5. **Create learning opportunities instead of sheltering teammates.** If the Conductor always solves the hard coordination problems, the specialist agents never learn to do it.
6. **Documents are leverage.** A well-written doc persuades, coordinates, and outlasts the meeting.

## Concrete templates, checklists, or artifacts the agent can reuse

**Project kickoff doc (Reilly-style), 5 sections:**
1. Why does this matter? (link to org priority)
2. Problem statement + current state
3. Proposed approach + alternatives considered
4. Scope — explicit in and out
5. Plan: milestones, owners, how we'll know it's done

**Glue-work audit (run weekly on the Conductor's own activity):**
- What coordination did I do this week that no one else could see?
- Did I write it down? (meeting notes, decision log, PR summary)
- Is any of this work I should be teaching a specialist agent to do instead?
- Did I ship any of my own artifacts this week, or was it all unblocking?

**Unblock-checklist (when a specialist agent stalls):**
1. Is the goal ambiguous? (fix with a written decision)
2. Is the dependency missing? (find/create it, or cut scope)
3. Is the decision above their scope? (escalate with a concrete recommendation, not an open question)
4. Is it actually blocked, or is it just uncomfortable? (different interventions)

**"Write the thing down" heuristic:** If the same question has come up twice in the Linear thread, stop answering inline — write an ADR or design note and link it.

## Where they disagree with others
- **Reilly vs. Larson:** Larson's archetypes can read as prescriptive lanes. Reilly is more explicit that staff work is mostly glue *regardless* of archetype, and that the boundary between "technical" and "coordination" work is largely cultural.
- **Reilly vs. Orosz:** Orosz emphasizes what senior engineers at top companies *do* (often individual technical output). Reilly emphasizes what they *should be credited for* (often invisible coordination). Useful corrective when reading perf-rubric advice.
